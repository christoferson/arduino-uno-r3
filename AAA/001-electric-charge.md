## 💡 The Big Idea

> **Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force when placed in an electromagnetic field.**

In simpler terms: **Charge is the "stuff" that makes electricity happen.**

---

## 📖 What is Electric Charge?

### The Atomic Picture

Everything around you is made of **atoms**. Each atom contains:

```
    Atom Structure

       e- (Electron, negative)
          |
          |
    +-----+-----+
    |           |
    |  Nucleus  |
    |  (p+, n0) |
    +-----------+
```

**Three key particles:**

| Particle | Charge | Location | Mass |
|----------|--------|----------|------|
| **Proton** | Positive (+) | Nucleus (center) | Heavy |
| **Neutron** | Neutral (0) | Nucleus (center) | Heavy |
| **Electron** | Negative (−) | Orbiting nucleus | Very light |

### The Key Insight 🔑

- **Protons** are stuck in the nucleus (they don't move easily)
- **Electrons** can move from atom to atom
- **When electrons move, we get electricity!**

---

## ⚖️ Positive vs. Negative Charge

### The Balance

Normally, atoms are **electrically neutral**:
- Number of protons (+) = Number of electrons (−)
- Charges cancel out: +3 and −3 = 0

### When Things Get Charged

**Negatively Charged Object:**

Extra electrons ⊖ ⊖ ⊖ ⊖ ⊖ More (−) than (+)

**Positively Charged Object:**

Missing electrons ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ ⊕ More (+) than (−)

### The Fundamental Rule

> **Like charges repel, opposite charges attract**

⊕ ←→ ⊕ Repel (push apart) ⊖ ←→ ⊖ Repel (push apart) ⊕ ←→ ⊖ Attract (pull together)

## 🌊 Water Analogy

Think of electric charge like **water molecules**:

| Electricity | Water System |
|-------------|--------------|
| Electric charge | Water molecules |
| Amount of charge | Amount of water |
| Charged object | Container of water |
| Electron flow | Water flow |

Just as you can measure water in gallons or liters, we measure charge in **Coulombs**.

---

## 🔢 Mathematical Definition

### The Coulomb (C)

The **Coulomb** is the SI unit of electric charge.

**Symbol:** C (capital C)

**Definition:**

1 Coulomb = charge of 6.242 × 10¹⁸ electrons

That's about **6,242,000,000,000,000,000 electrons!**

### The Elementary Charge

The charge of a single electron:

e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ Coulombs

This is incredibly tiny! That's why we need billions of billions of electrons to make 1 Coulomb.

### Charge Formula

Q = n × e

Where: Q = Total charge (Coulombs) n = Number of electrons e = Elementary charge (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)

---

## 📏 Measuring Charge

### Typical Charge Values

| Scenario | Approximate Charge |
|----------|-------------------|
| Static shock | 0.001 C (1 mC) |
| Lightning bolt | 15 C |
| Car battery (total) | 180,000 C |
| Single electron | 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C |

### Charge Notation

- **Positive charge:** Q = +5 C
- **Negative charge:** Q = −5 C
- **Neutral:** Q = 0 C

## 🧪 Practical Examples

### Example 1: Rubbing a Balloon

**What happens:**
1. You rub a balloon on your hair
2. Electrons transfer from your hair to the balloon
3. Balloon becomes negatively charged (excess electrons)
4. Your hair becomes positively charged (missing electrons)
5. Balloon sticks to wall (opposite charges attract)

Before: After: Hair: neutral Hair: ⊕⊕⊕ (positive) Balloon: neutral Balloon: ⊖⊖⊖ (negative)

### Example 2: Lightning

**The process:**
1. Clouds accumulate charge through friction
2. Bottom of cloud: negative (−)
3. Ground below: positive (+)
4. When charge difference is huge: **LIGHTNING!**
5. Electrons rush from cloud to ground

**Charge transferred:** ~15 Coulombs in 0.001 seconds!

### Example 3: Battery

A typical AA battery doesn't "contain" charge like water in a bottle. Instead:
- It has a **chemical imbalance**
- One end has excess electrons (−)
- Other end has deficit of electrons (+)
- When connected, electrons flow to balance out

## 🔄 Static vs. Flowing Charge

### Static Charge (Stationary)

**Characteristics:**
- Charge builds up on objects
- Doesn't flow continuously
- Examples: rubbed balloon, static shock, charged comb


Object with static charge: ⊖⊖⊖⊖⊖⊖⊖⊖ (electrons just sitting there)

### Flowing Charge (Current)

**Characteristics:**
- Charge moves through a conductor
- Continuous flow
- This is what we call **electric current** (next lesson!)


Wire with flowing charge: ⊖→⊖→⊖→⊖→⊖→⊖→ (electrons moving)

## ⚡ Common Misconceptions

### ❌ Misconception 1: "Electricity is consumed"

**Reality:** Charge is **conserved**. Electrons aren't destroyed; they just move around. What's consumed is **energy**, not charge.

### ❌ Misconception 2: "Current flows from + to −"

**Reality:** 
- **Electrons** (negative) flow from − to +
- **Conventional current** (historical convention) flows from + to −
- Both are correct depending on context!

### ❌ Misconception 3: "Positive charge means adding protons"

**Reality:** Protons don't move easily. Positive charge usually means **removing electrons**, not adding protons.

### ❌ Misconception 4: "Batteries store charge"

**Reality:** Batteries store **chemical energy** that can push charge through a circuit. The charge itself comes from the electrons already in the wires.

## ✅ Self-Check Questions

### Basic Understanding

1. **What is electric charge?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   A fundamental property of matter caused by the presence or absence of electrons.
   </details>

2. **Which particle is responsible for most electrical phenomena?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   The electron (because it can move easily between atoms).
   </details>

3. **What does it mean when an object is "positively charged"?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   It has fewer electrons than protons (missing electrons).
   </details>

### Calculations

4. **How many electrons are in 1 Coulomb of charge?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   6.242 × 10¹⁸ electrons
   </details>

5. **If an object has 3.204 × 10¹⁹ excess electrons, what is its charge?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

   Q = n × e
   Q = (3.204 × 10¹⁹) × (1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹)
   Q = 5.13 C (negative charge)

   Answer: −5.13 C
   </details>

### Conceptual

6. **Two balloons are both negatively charged. What happens when you bring them close together?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   They repel each other (like charges repel).
   </details>

7. **Why does a charged balloon stick to a neutral wall?**
   <details>
   <summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>
   The charged balloon induces a separation of charges in the wall (polarization), creating an attractive force.
   </details>

## 🔬 Try It Yourself

### Experiment 1: Static Electricity
**Materials:** Balloon, your hair, small pieces of paper

**Steps:**
1. Rub balloon on your hair for 30 seconds
2. Bring balloon near small paper pieces
3. Observe what happens

**What's happening:** You're transferring electrons, creating charge imbalance!

### Experiment 2: Charge Detection
**Materials:** Two strips of clear tape

**Steps:**
1. Stick two pieces of tape on a table
2. Quickly pull them off
3. Bring them near each other

**What's happening:** Both strips get the same charge (both positive or both negative), so they repel!

## 🎓 Key Takeaways

1. ⚡ **Charge is a fundamental property** caused by electrons
2. 🔋 **Two types:** Positive (+) and Negative (−)
3. 🧲 **Like charges repel, opposites attract**
4. 📏 **Unit:** Coulomb (C)
5. 🔢 **1 C = 6.242 × 10¹⁸ electrons**
6. 🎯 **Electrons move, protons don't** (in most cases)
